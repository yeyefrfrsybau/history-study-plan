Paper 1 — Section I

Pre-Independence Era

Mughal decline, British arrival, 1857 War, Islamic reformers, Sir Syed Ahmed Khan

🔥 Very Hot Decline of the Mughal Empire +
What you need to know

The Mughal Empire reached its peak under Aurangzeb (died 1707), but his policies sowed the seeds of decline. After his death, a rapid succession of weak emperors allowed regional powers to fill the vacuum.

Key causes of decline:

  • Weak successors — after Aurangzeb, 12 emperors ruled in just 30 years. None had political skill or military strength.
  • Aurangzeb's religious policies — reimposing the jizya tax on Hindus and destroying temples alienated the Hindu population and fuelled the Maratha revolt.
  • Maratha rise — Shivaji and later Maratha confederacy carved away large portions of the Deccan and central India.
  • Foreign invasions — Nadir Shah of Persia sacked Delhi in 1739, taking the Peacock Throne and massacring 30,000 people. Ahmad Shah Durrani (Afghanistan) invaded nine times between 1748–1767, each time weakening central authority further.
  • Economic collapse — constant wars drained the treasury. Provincial governors (nawabs) stopped sending revenue to Delhi and became independent rulers.
  • Military decay — the army relied on outdated weapons and tactics. European-trained forces (EIC sepoys) were far superior.
  • Arrival of the EIC — the British East India Company exploited political fragmentation, playing nawabs against each other through a policy of divide and rule.
How questions appear
3 marks Source-based (Section A)
"According to Source A, what happened after the death of Aurangzeb?"
7 marks Section B
"Why did the Mughal Empire decline after 1707?"
14 marks Section B evaluation
"Was Aurangzeb's reign the main reason for the decline of the Mughal Empire? Explain your answer."
How to answer — strategy
3-mark source question
1
Make 3 separate points directly from the source text. Do not add outside knowledge.
2
Use the phrasing "According to the source..." or "The source states that..."
3
Each point = 1 mark. Keep it brief — one sentence each.
7-mark "why" question
1
Write 3–4 developed paragraphs, each on a different cause.
2
Each paragraph: name the cause → explain it → show its consequence. E.g. "Weak successors meant that..." "This led to..."
3
No introduction needed. No conclusion needed. Just well-developed points.
14-mark evaluation question
1
Brief intro — set the scene (1–2 sentences only).
2
Argue FOR the statement with 2–3 developed points (e.g. yes, Aurangzeb's policies were important because...).
3
Argue AGAINST with 2–3 other causes (weak successors, foreign invasions, EIC).
4
Conclusion: make a judgment — "Overall, the most important reason was... because..." and justify it clearly.
Exam tip: For 14-mark questions, the conclusion is where top marks are won. Cambridge rewards a clear, reasoned judgment — not just "there were many reasons."
🔥 Very Hot War of Independence 1857 +
Causes
  • Military grievances (Immediate cause) — the introduction of the Enfield rifle cartridges greased with cow and pig fat deeply offended both Hindu and Muslim sepoys. Soldiers had to bite the cartridge to load — this was seen as a direct attack on their religion.
  • Political grievances — the Doctrine of Lapse (Dalhousie) meant Indian kingdoms without a male heir were annexed by the British. Nawabs like Wajid Ali Shah of Awadh were deposed, humiliating the Muslim aristocracy.
  • Economic grievances — British imported manufactured goods destroyed local Indian industries. Heavy land taxation left peasants in poverty. Indians were excluded from senior government and military positions.
  • Social/Religious grievances — Christian missionaries were active and feared. The British abolished practices like Suttee — seen as interference in religion and culture. Introduction of Western education threatened traditional Muslim scholarship.
  • Growing resentment — the "General Service Enlistment Act" required sepoys to serve overseas, which many considered religiously polluting.
Key events
  • May 1857 — Meerut: 85 sepoys refused to use cartridges, were court-martialled. Fellow sepoys rebelled, killed British officers, marched to Delhi.
  • Delhi: Rebels declared the last Mughal emperor Bahadur Shah Zafar as their leader.
  • Cawnpore (Kanpur): Major siege and massacre — became a rallying point for British retaliation.
  • Lucknow: Prolonged British Residency siege; held out for months.
  • British retook Delhi by September 1857 — the rebellion was largely suppressed by mid-1858.
Reasons for failure
  • Lack of unified leadership — Bahadur Shah Zafar was old and ineffective. There was no coordinated national command.
  • Limited geography — revolt was concentrated in UP, Delhi, Bihar. Punjab, Bengal, Madras did not join. Sikh soldiers actually helped the British.
  • Military superiority of British — better weapons, organisation, and the telegraph allowed rapid communication and troop movement.
  • Indian princes stayed loyal — many Indian rulers supported Britain and provided troops and resources.
  • No common goal — Hindus and Muslims had different aims; there was no shared vision of what to replace British rule with.
Results / Consequences
  • The East India Company was dissolved — direct British Crown rule began (British Raj, 1858).
  • Bahadur Shah Zafar was exiled to Burma — end of the Mughal dynasty.
  • Muslims were particularly blamed and faced severe reprisals, losing land, jobs, and influence.
  • The British reorganised the Indian Army — never again would Indians outnumber British troops so greatly.
  • The event inspired later Muslim reformers (especially Sir Syed Ahmed Khan) to rethink how Muslims should relate to British power.
How questions appear
5 marks Source-based
"What does Source B tell us about the War of Independence?"
7 marks
"Why did the War of Independence fail?" / "Why did the War of Independence happen in 1857?"
10 marks Section A evaluation
"Economic factors were more important than military factors leading to 1857. Do you agree?"
14 marks
"How successful was the War of Independence?" / "Was the cartridge issue the main cause of the 1857 War?"
Answer strategy — 5-mark source question
1
Make 5 distinct points from what the source shows (image/text). For images, describe what you see AND what it suggests/implies.
2
E.g. for a battle scene image: "The source shows fighting between Indian soldiers and British forces, suggesting the scale of the conflict..."
3
Always link the visual detail to its historical significance.
Exam tip: For the 10-mark Section A question (d), you must present BOTH sides of the argument before giving your conclusion. Argue FOR the statement, then AGAINST, then judge. One-sided answers are capped at around 6 marks.
★ Key Shah Waliullah & Islamic Reformers +
The three reformers — who they are

Cambridge expects you to know Shah Waliullah in most depth, but also be aware of Syed Ahmad Barelvi (Jihad Movement) and Haji Shariat Ullah (Faraezi Movement).

Shah Waliullah (1703–1762)
  • Background: Born in Delhi, studied in Mecca and Medina. Returned determined to revive Islam in the subcontinent amid Mughal decline.
  • Translation of the Quran: Translated the Quran into Persian so more Muslims could understand it directly — revolutionary at the time. His sons later translated it into Urdu.
  • Unifying sects: Tried to reconcile divisions between Sunni schools of thought (Hanafi, Shafi, etc.) to create Muslim unity.
  • Political vision: Invited Ahmad Shah Durrani to invade India and crush the Marathas — leading to the Third Battle of Panipat (1761), which temporarily halted Maratha expansion.
  • Education reform: Emphasised the study of Hadith and rational Islamic sciences. His school produced a generation of reformist scholars.
  • Legacy: His ideas directly inspired both the Jihad Movement and later the Deoband school of thought, shaping Muslim identity in South Asia for centuries.
Syed Ahmad Barelvi — Jihad Movement
  • Student of Shah Waliullah's son. Preached return to pure Islamic practice.
  • Organised armed jihad against the Sikh Kingdom in Punjab (not against the British initially).
  • Killed at the Battle of Balakot (1831) — the movement lost its leader but influenced later Muslim activism.
  • Why it failed: Poor military planning, lack of local Pashtun support who switched sides, no unified command after Barelvi's death.
Haji Shariat Ullah — Faraezi Movement (Bengal)
  • Focused on Bengal's Muslim peasants, who were exploited by Hindu landlords and British indigo planters.
  • Declared that Bengal under British rule was Dar-ul-Harb (land of war), so Friday prayers and Eid prayers (which require a free Islamic state) should not be held.
  • Emphasised the Faraiz (obligatory duties of Islam) as a way to purify and unite Bengali Muslims.
  • Combined religious revival with economic protest against exploitation.

4 marks
"Describe the work of Shah Waliullah."
7 marks
"Why did Syed Ahmad Barelvi's Jihad Movement fail?"
14 marks
"Did Shah Waliullah contribute more to the spread of Islam than anyone else before 1850? Explain your answer."
Answer strategy — 4-mark describe
1
Make 4 separate factual points. Each point must include specific detail (not just "he translated the Quran" — say INTO WHICH LANGUAGE and WHY it mattered).
2
No argument or evaluation needed. Just accurate, detailed description.
Exam tip: When comparing reformers in a 14-mark question, use this structure: argue that SWU was most important (2–3 points), then consider the OTHER reformers' contributions, then conclude with a justified judgment. Saying "all were equally important" earns low marks — you must pick one and defend it.
★ Key Sir Syed Ahmed Khan — Aligarh Movement & Two-Nation Theory +
Context & motivation

After 1857, Muslims were devastated — blamed by the British, stripped of land and jobs, and educationally backward. Sir Syed, a Muslim civil servant who had loyally served the British, concluded that Muslims' only path forward was cooperation with — not resistance to — British rule, combined with modern education.

Key contributions
  • MAO School, Aligarh (1875) → MAO College (1877): Founded on Oxford/Cambridge model. Taught both modern Western sciences AND Islamic studies. Aimed to produce loyal, educated Muslim leaders who could compete for government positions.
  • Mohammadan Educational Conference (1886): Travelling educational reform movement that spread schools across Muslim communities.
  • The Causes of the Indian Revolt (1859): A book defending Muslims — argued the revolt was caused by British misgovernance, not Muslim treachery. Helped improve British attitudes toward Muslims.
  • Promoted Urdu: Championed Urdu as the language of educated Muslims, opposing Hindu leaders who pushed for Hindi in Persian/Arabic script to be replaced.
  • Two-Nation Theory: Argued that Hindus and Muslims were two separate nations with different religions, cultures, laws, and histories. They could not coexist under one democratic government where Hindus' numerical majority would always dominate Muslims. This idea later became the intellectual foundation for Pakistan.
  • Opposed Congress: Advised Muslims not to join the Indian National Congress (founded 1885), arguing it represented Hindu interests.
How questions appear
7 marks
"Why did Sir Syed Ahmed Khan try to improve relations between Muslims and the British?"
7 marks
"Why did Sir Syed Ahmed Khan oppose the Indian National Congress?"
14 marks
"Was Aligarh College the most important of Sir Syed Ahmed Khan's contributions? Explain your answer."
Exam tip: Sir Syed often appears as a 7-mark question in Section B. Cambridge rewards you for showing the connection between his actions and their context — why he did things, not just what he did.
Paper 1 — Section II

The Pakistan Movement (1905–1947)

Partition of Bengal · Muslim League · Khilafat · Simon Commission · Congress Rule · Lahore Resolution

🔥 Very Hot Partition of Bengal (1905) & Reversal (1911) +
Why Bengal was partitioned (1905)
  • Administrative reasons: Bengal was the largest province — 78 million people. Lord Curzon argued it was too large to govern effectively.
  • Muslim benefit: The new Eastern Bengal & Assam had a Muslim majority. Dhaka became its capital. Muslim leaders welcomed the economic opportunity and political voice this gave them.
  • British strategy: Many historians argue Curzon also aimed to divide the Hindu-Muslim alliance that was growing in the nationalist movement — classic divide and rule.
Hindu reaction — Swadeshi Movement
  • Hindu leaders were furious — they saw Bengal as their cultural heartland being broken up.
  • Launched the Swadeshi Movement: boycott of British goods, burning of British cloth, promotion of Indian-made goods.
  • Mass protests, hartals (strikes), and political agitation across India.
Reversal (1911) & Muslim reaction
  • Under intense Hindu pressure, the British King George V announced the re-unification of Bengal at the Delhi Durbar (1911).
  • Muslims felt betrayed — they had supported the British and received nothing. This reinforced the lesson that they needed their own political organisation to protect their interests.
  • The reversal accelerated the growth and seriousness of the All-India Muslim League, founded just 5 years earlier.
Formation of Muslim League (1906)
  • The Simla Deputation (1906): 35 Muslim leaders, led by the Aga Khan, met Viceroy Minto to demand separate Muslim electorates — Muslims should only be voted for by Muslim voters. Minto agreed in principle.
  • At Dhaka in December 1906, the All-India Muslim League was formally established under Nawab Salimullah Khan. Its aims: protect Muslim political rights, promote loyalty to the British Crown, and prevent anti-Muslim sentiment.
  • Morley-Minto Reforms (1909): Gave Muslims separate electorates — a major victory. But Congress condemned it as divisive.
How questions appear
4 marks
"Describe the terms of the Morley-Minto Reforms of 1909."
7 marks
"Why did the partition of Bengal lead to the formation of the Muslim League?"
14 marks
"Was the Partition of Bengal the main reason for the development of a new political party in India in 1906?"
Exam tip: The 14-mark question will offer a named reason (Partition of Bengal). You should argue YES it was important, but also bring in other reasons (Swadeshi threat to Muslims, need for separate electorates, Congress domination) and then decide which was most important.
🔥 Very Hot Khilafat Movement (1919–1924) +
Background & causes
  • The Ottoman Sultan was the Caliph — spiritual leader of all Sunni Muslims worldwide. After WWI, Britain and its allies planned to dismember the Ottoman Empire and abolish the Caliphate.
  • Indian Muslims felt this was an attack on Islam itself. They had loyally supported Britain in WWI expecting consideration — this felt like betrayal.
  • The Ali Brothers (Muhammad Ali and Shaukat Ali) launched the Khilafat Movement to pressure Britain to preserve the Caliphate.
  • Gandhi allied with the Khilafat Movement — creating a rare Hindu-Muslim united front against British rule (Non-Cooperation Movement).
Why it failed
  • Abolition of the Caliphate by Turks themselves: In 1924, Mustafa Kemal Atatürk abolished the Ottoman Caliphate to modernise Turkey. The cause the movement was fighting for no longer existed — the movement instantly lost its purpose.
  • Chauri Chaura incident (1922): A mob burned a police station, killing 22 officers. Gandhi unilaterally suspended the Non-Cooperation Movement. Muslims felt abandoned by their main ally.
  • Hindu-Muslim tensions: The alliance was fragile. Violence between communities (Moplah Rebellion in Kerala) eroded trust.
  • British suppression: The Ali Brothers and other leaders were arrested. The movement lost its organisational leadership.
  • No political gains: Britain did not change its policy toward Turkey. The movement achieved none of its original aims.
Results / Significance
  • First major mass political movement of Indian Muslims — showed their capacity for political mobilisation.
  • Deepened Muslim distrust of Congress and Gandhi after the sudden suspension.
  • After 1924, Muslim-Hindu relations deteriorated significantly — the era of cooperation was over.
  • Pushed Muslims toward developing their own separate political identity — the next decade saw growing demand for Muslim-only political solutions.
How questions appear
7 marks
"Why did the Khilafat Movement fail?"
7 marks
"Why was the Khilafat Movement started?"
14 marks
"Which affected Hindu-Muslim relations the most — Partition of Bengal, Montague-Chelmsford Reforms, or Nehru Report?"
7-mark answer structure for "Why did it fail?"
1
Open with the most important reason (Turks abolishing the Caliphate themselves). Explain fully.
2
Add 2–3 other reasons (Chauri Chaura, Hindu-Muslim tensions, British suppression) each in a developed paragraph.
3
Each paragraph: state the reason → explain HOW it caused failure → show consequence.
🔥 Very Hot Simon Commission & Nehru Report (1927–1929) +
Simon Commission (1927)
  • Appointed by the British government to review the 1919 Montague-Chelmsford Reforms and recommend constitutional changes for India.
  • The problem: The commission had NO Indian members — all 7 members were British MPs. Both Congress and Muslim League opposed it as a denial of Indian rights to decide their own future.
  • Greeted with "Simon Go Back" protests everywhere it visited.
  • Jinnah condemned it but also used it to push for Muslim demands: separate electorates, one-third Muslim representation in central legislature, residual powers to provinces.
Nehru Report (1928)
  • Congress responded to Simon Commission by drafting their own constitutional proposals, led by Motilal Nehru.
  • Key proposals that angered Muslims: Joint electorates (ending separate Muslim electorates), residual powers to the centre (not provinces — which would mean Hindu majority central government controlled everything), no reservation of Muslim seats in Muslim-minority provinces.
  • Jinnah offered amendments to make it acceptable (his "Delhi Proposals" / Fourteen Points) but Congress rejected them.
  • This was a turning point — Jinnah called it the "parting of ways." He had spent 20 years trying to build Hindu-Muslim unity; the Nehru Report convinced him it was impossible.
Jinnah's Fourteen Points (1929)
  • Jinnah's response to the Nehru Report — his minimum demands for Muslim participation in a united India's constitution.
  • Key points: federal constitution with residual powers to provinces; separate electorates until Muslims chose to abandon them; one-third Muslim representation in central legislature; religious freedom guaranteed; no territorial changes without provincial assembly consent.
  • Congress rejected these. This failure ended Jinnah's hope for a united India solution and pushed him toward the two-nation approach.
How questions appear
3 marks Source A
"According to Source A, why was there opposition to the Simon Commission?"
7 marks
"Why did the All-India Muslim League reject the Nehru Report in 1928?"
10 marks
"How successful were political developments in finding a solution between 1909 and 1919?"
🔥 Very Hot Congress Rule, 1935 Act & Lahore Resolution 1940 +
Government of India Act 1935
  • Introduced provincial autonomy — Indians could govern provinces. The central government remained under British control.
  • Created a federation of British India and Princely States (never fully implemented).
  • Importance: first time real governmental power was given to elected Indian ministers. Set the stage for the 1937 elections.
  • Both Congress and Muslim League opposed aspects of it — Congress wanted full independence, Muslims wanted stronger provincial autonomy safeguards.
Congress Rule (1937–1939)
  • Congress won a majority in 7 of 11 provinces in the 1937 elections. Muslim League did poorly — won only 25% of reserved Muslim seats.
  • Congress refused coalition government with the League, insisting the League dissolve its identity into Congress.
  • Muslim grievances under Congress Rule: Vande Mataram (a Hindu nationalist song) was made the national song; Hindi was promoted; Wardha Scheme for education promoted Hindu culture; Muslims faced discrimination in jobs and civil service; cow protection laws passed; mosques facing interference.
  • Jinnah declared Day of Deliverance (22 December 1939) when Congress resigned after Britain declared war without consulting Indian leaders. Muslims celebrated Congress leaving power.
  • The Congress Rule experience was decisive — it showed Muslims what majority Hindu rule would look like. Support for the Muslim League surged massively.
Lahore Resolution 1940
  • Passed on 23 March 1940 at the All-India Muslim League annual session in Lahore.
  • Demanded that the areas in the north-west and east of India where Muslims were in majority should be grouped to constitute independent states (note: plural — not "one Pakistan").
  • Did not use the word "Pakistan" (coined by Choudhry Rahmat Ali in 1933 in Cambridge).
  • Allama Iqbal's 1930 Allahabad Address had earlier envisioned a separate Muslim homeland in north-west India — the intellectual forerunner.
  • Significance: turned the vague idea of a Muslim homeland into an official political demand. The Pakistan Movement now had a clear goal.
1940–1947 key events
  • Cripps Mission (1942): British offered Dominion status post-war — Congress rejected it ("a post-dated cheque on a failing bank"); Jinnah rejected it because it didn't guarantee a separate Muslim state.
  • Quit India Movement (1942): Gandhi launched mass civil disobedience. British arrested all Congress leaders. This actually helped the Muslim League — they cooperated with Britain and gained credibility.
  • Shimla Conference (1945): Failed — Congress claimed to represent all Indians; Jinnah insisted only the League could speak for Muslims.
  • 1945 Elections: Muslim League won every single Muslim seat in the central legislature — a massive mandate for Pakistan.
  • Cabinet Mission Plan (1946): Proposed a three-tier federal structure keeping India united but with Muslim-majority groups having autonomy. League accepted then rejected; Congress rejected key parts. Plan collapsed.
  • Direct Action Day (16 Aug 1946): League called for strikes; massive Hindu-Muslim riots in Calcutta, thousands killed. Showed partition was becoming inevitable.
  • June 3rd Plan (Mountbatten Plan, 1947): British announced India would be partitioned and both nations given independence. Punjab and Bengal would be divided.
  • Independence: 14 August 1947 — Pakistan; 15 August 1947 — India.
How questions appear
7 marks
"Was the Day of Deliverance justified?" / "Why was the Lahore Resolution passed in 1940?"
14 marks
"How successful were negotiations aimed at independence during the Second World War?"
10 marks Section A
"Was Congress Rule the most important reason for the creation of Pakistan? Do you agree?"
Exam tip: "Was the Day of Deliverance justified?" is a classic 7-mark question. Write 3–4 reasons arguing YES it was justified (Muslim grievances under Congress Rule), then briefly present the counter-argument (Congress did bring some development), then give your view. Your stance must be clear.
Paper 1 — Section III

Post-Independence Pakistan

Early problems · Governors-General · Bangladesh · Zia · Bhutto · Constitutions

🔥 Very Hot Early Problems of Pakistan (1947–1948) +
The problems
  • Refugee crisis: 7–10 million Muslims crossed into Pakistan; equal numbers of Hindus and Sikhs fled to India. No infrastructure to absorb them. Disease, starvation, and violence killed hundreds of thousands during the migration.
  • Division of assets: India was given the bulk of the British Indian Army, civil service, treasury, and equipment. Pakistan was supposed to get 17.5% of the British Indian treasury (£75 million) — India delayed and reduced the payment. Pakistan received far less military hardware than agreed.
  • Kashmir dispute: The Hindu Maharaja of Kashmir Hari Singh hesitated — then acceded to India in October 1947 as Pakistani-backed tribal fighters invaded. Pakistan refused to accept Indian accession. The first Kashmir War began immediately. A UN ceasefire left Kashmir divided.
  • Canal Water dispute: The headwaters of rivers feeding Pakistan's Punjab were in India. India cut water flow to Pakistani canals on 1 April 1948 — crippling agriculture for weeks. Resolved temporarily by the Inter-Dominion Agreement (1948), permanently by the Indus Waters Treaty (1960).
  • Capital problem: Pakistan had no capital ready. Karachi was designated but lacked government buildings, infrastructure, or facilities for a functioning capital.
  • No constitution: Pakistan had no constitution of its own — continued under the adapted Government of India Act 1935, a temporary and unsatisfactory arrangement.
  • Economic weakness: Pakistan inherited mostly agricultural areas; major industries, ports, and financial infrastructure were in India. Karachi was the only port. Most of the skilled Hindu business community had fled to India.
How questions appear
7 marks
"Why was there a refugee crisis in 1947–48?" / "Why was there a Canal Water dispute?"
14 marks
"How successful was Pakistan in solving the problems of Partition during 1947 and 1948?"
14-mark "how successful" structure
1
Successes: what problems were resolved (Canal Water treaty, ceasefire in Kashmir, refugees gradually settled, Liaquat Ali Khan built a functioning civil service).
2
Failures: what remained unresolved (Kashmir, lack of constitution, economic weakness, refugee rehabilitation incomplete).
3
Conclusion: overall judgment — "Pakistan was partially successful but..." with specific evidence.
🔥 Very Hot Liaquat Ali Khan, Ghulam Muhammad & Iskander Mirza +
Liaquat Ali Khan (PM 1947–1951)
  • Objectives Resolution (1949): Declared Pakistan would be governed on Islamic principles — foundation for all future constitutions.
  • Stabilised the refugee crisis through major administrative effort.
  • Maintained relations with both the US and the non-aligned world.
  • Negotiated the Liaquat-Nehru Pact (1950) on minority rights.
  • Assassinated October 1951 at Rawalpindi — killer shot dead immediately, conspiracy never fully explained. His death plunged Pakistan into political instability.
Malik Ghulam Muhammad (Governor-General 1951–1955)
  • A bureaucrat, not a politician. Treated Pakistan as a personal domain.
  • Dismissed Prime Ministers at will — dismissed Khwaja Nazimuddin in 1953 despite him having a parliamentary majority (unconstitutional).
  • Maulvi Tamizuddin Case (1954): Dismissed the Constituent Assembly when it tried to limit his powers. Pakistan's Federal Court controversially upheld this.
  • His arbitrary use of power severely damaged Pakistan's democratic traditions at the crucial formative stage.
  • Introduced One Unit Scheme (1955) — merged all West Pakistan provinces into one unit, partly to balance East Pakistan's population advantage.
Iskander Mirza (President 1956–1958)
  • Pakistan finally got its first constitution in 1956 — declared Pakistan an Islamic Republic.
  • Mirza constantly manipulated politics — dismissed 4 prime ministers in 2 years. He engineered political crises to maintain personal power.
  • Martial Law — October 1958: Mirza abrogated the constitution, imposed martial law, and appointed General Ayub Khan as Chief Martial Law Administrator — thinking he could control him.
  • Just 20 days later, Ayub Khan deposed Mirza himself and exiled him to London. Pakistan's first military takeover was complete.
How questions appear
4 marks
"Describe the constitutional crisis during 1954–55."
7 marks
"Why was there a constitutional crisis in 1958?"
14 marks
"Which contributed most to Pakistan's problems between 1948 and 1958 — Liaquat Ali Khan, Ghulam Muhammad, or Iskander Mirza?"
Exam tip: For the 3-way comparison 14-mark question, do not write equal amounts on each person — that suggests you have no judgment. Argue a case: most candidates find Ghulam Muhammad the most damaging (dismissed elected governments unconstitutionally, undermined democracy at its foundation). Back your choice with evidence.
🔥 Very Hot Creation of Bangladesh (1971) +
Grievances of East Pakistan
  • Language issue: Government declared Urdu the only national language — Bengalis (East Pakistan's majority) were furious. Bengali Language Movement (1952) — students shot dead on 21 February, now International Mother Language Day.
  • Economic discrimination: East Pakistan generated most of Pakistan's foreign exchange (jute exports) but received less development spending. West Pakistan got the lion's share of industry, infrastructure, and government investment.
  • Political under-representation: Despite having a larger population, East Pakistan was under-represented in the civil service, military, and federal government — dominated by West Pakistanis.
  • Geographical separation: 1,000 miles of India between the two wings made unified governance difficult and East Pakistanis felt remote and neglected.
  • Cultural difference: Bengalis had a distinct language, literature, and cultural identity that West Pakistani leaders often dismissed or tried to suppress.
  • One Unit Scheme: Merged West Pakistan provinces — giving West Pakistan parity with East despite East's larger population. Seen as another injustice.
The crisis of 1970–71
  • 1970 elections: Awami League (Mujibur Rahman) won a massive majority — enough to form government alone. PPP (Bhutto) won in West Pakistan. Bhutto refused to accept Mujib as PM; Yahya Khan delayed transfer of power.
  • Operation Searchlight (March 1971): Pakistan Army launched a brutal military crackdown in East Pakistan — targeting Awami League supporters, intellectuals, Hindus. Thousands killed. Mujib arrested.
  • War and Indian intervention: 10 million refugees fled to India. India trained Mukti Bahini guerrillas and eventually entered the war directly (December 1971).
  • Surrender (16 December 1971): Pakistani forces surrendered in Dhaka — 93,000 Pakistani soldiers became prisoners of war. Bangladesh declared independence.
How questions appear
7 marks
"Why did East Pakistan separate from West Pakistan in 1971?"
14 marks
"Was the language issue the main reason for the separation of East Pakistan? Explain your answer."
🔥 Very Hot General Zia ul-Haq (1977–1988) +
How he came to power
  • Bhutto's PPP won 1977 elections but opposition alleged massive rigging. Protests paralysed the country.
  • General Zia ul-Haq (Army Chief appointed by Bhutto) launched a coup on 5 July 1977 — Operation Fair Play.
  • Bhutto was arrested, tried for murder, and hanged in April 1979 — a deeply controversial decision.
Islamisation policies
  • Hudood Ordinances (1979): Introduced punishments prescribed by Islamic law (hadd) — flogging for alcohol, amputation for theft, stoning for adultery. Highly controversial; widely criticised for being applied selectively against women and minorities.
  • Zakat and Ushr: Compulsory Islamic tax deducted from bank accounts. Shia Muslims protested violently and were exempted.
  • Islamisation of economy: Interest (riba) was officially prohibited; replaced with profit-sharing schemes. In practice largely ineffective.
  • Education reform: Islamic studies made compulsory. Madrasa education expanded massively.
  • Legal changes: Law of Evidence (Qanun-e-Shahadat) gave women's testimony half the weight of men's in certain cases. Blasphemy laws strengthened (Section 295-C).
  • Media censorship: Strict censorship imposed. Women newsreaders required to cover their heads.
Afghan War and US relations
  • Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979) transformed Pakistan's geopolitical importance.
  • Pakistan became the frontline state for US-CIA weapons and funding to Afghan Mujahideen. Billions in aid flooded in.
  • Millions of Afghan refugees entered Pakistan. Gun and drug culture spread widely.
  • Zia used the Afghan war to legitimise his rule and obtain international support, delaying pressure for democracy.
Assessment — successes and failures
  • Successes: Brought political stability after Bhutto era chaos; economic growth was reasonable; nuclear programme advanced; Afghan war brought massive aid; Islamic identity of Pakistan strengthened for some.
  • Failures: Democracy suppressed for 11 years; human rights abused; sectarian violence increased; women's rights set back; gun/drug culture spread; blasphemy laws misused; press freedom eliminated.
How questions appear
7 marks
"Why did Zia ul-Haq find it difficult to rule Pakistan during the late 1980s?"
14 marks
"Was the promotion of Islamic values the most important achievement of Zia ul-Haq's domestic policies 1977–1988?"
Exam tip: Zia is a favourite 14-mark question. The named factor (Islamic values) is the one you must evaluate. Argue YES it was important (Islamisation policies listed above), then argue NO — other achievements matter too (political stability, economic growth, Afghan strategy). Conclude with a clear verdict.
Paper 1 — Foreign Policy

Pakistan's Foreign Relations

India · USA · USSR/China · UN · Nuclear programme

🔥 Very Hot Relations with India & Kashmir Issue +
Key phases
  • First Kashmir War (1947–48): Tribal fighters entered Kashmir; Maharaja acceded to India. UN brokered ceasefire; Kashmir divided at Line of Control. UN resolution promised a plebiscite — never held.
  • 1965 War: Operation Gibraltar — Pakistan sent irregular forces into Kashmir hoping a mass uprising would follow. India crossed the international border into Punjab. 17 days of heavy fighting. USSR-brokered Tashkent Agreement (1966) — both sides returned to pre-war positions. Seen as a stalemate; Ayub Khan's reputation damaged.
  • 1971 War: Pakistani army surrendered in Dhaka. India was the dominant power. Simla Agreement (1972) — bilateral negotiations only; Line of Control in Kashmir formally accepted. Pakistan recognised Bangladesh.
  • Kargil Crisis (1999): Pakistani forces occupied Kargil peaks in Indian-controlled Kashmir. Intense fighting; international pressure (especially US) forced Pakistan to withdraw. Brought both nuclear-armed countries to the brink of war.
Periods of engagement
  • Composite Dialogue processes attempted in 1990s under Nawaz Sharif and Vajpayee — made limited progress.
  • Water sharing: Indus Waters Treaty (1960) — World Bank brokered; generally held even during wars.
  • Trade: always limited and politically driven. Normalisation repeatedly tried and abandoned.

★ Key Relations with USA, USSR, China & Muslim World +
United States
  • Pakistan joined SEATO (1954) and CENTO (1955) — American-led anti-communist alliances. Received military and economic aid.
  • Relations cooled in 1965 when US cut aid during the India-Pakistan war.
  • Nixon-Kissinger used Pakistan as back-channel to open relations with China (1971) — Pakistan played a vital intermediary role.
  • Worst point: 1979 when Pakistan's US Embassy was burned during anti-American protests following the Iranian Revolution.
  • Afghan War (1979–89) — Pakistan became America's most important ally. Billions in aid, weapons, and CIA cooperation against the Soviets.
  • Post-Cold War: US imposed sanctions when Pakistan tested nuclear weapons (1998). Relations deteriorated sharply.
USSR / Russia
  • Pakistan's pro-Western stance (SEATO/CENTO) created immediate tensions with the USSR.
  • USSR supported India — supplied weapons, vetoed UN resolutions on Kashmir.
  • 1966 Tashkent Agreement — positive moment, USSR mediated peace after 1965 war.
  • Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979) made USSR Pakistan's direct adversary. Pakistan sheltered Mujahideen.
  • After Soviet withdrawal (1989), relations gradually normalised.
China
  • Relations began poorly — both fought on opposite sides in the Cold War.
  • Turned strongly positive from early 1960s. Border agreement (1963) — Pakistan ceded some Kashmir territory to China; China gave territorial recognition.
  • China became Pakistan's most reliable long-term ally — consistently supported Pakistan at UN on Kashmir, provided military technology, helped build Pakistan's nuclear programme.
  • The "all-weather friendship" — described as "higher than mountains, deeper than oceans."
  • China vetoed multiple UN resolutions against Pakistan. Modern CPEC (China-Pakistan Economic Corridor) is the latest manifestation.
Muslim World & UN
  • Pakistan was founding member of the United Nations (1947) — needed international recognition and legitimacy, a forum for Kashmir, and access to economic institutions.
  • Active in OIC (Organisation of Islamic Cooperation). Hosted OIC summit in Lahore 1974.
  • Supported Palestinian cause consistently. Refused to recognise Israel.
  • Relations with Iran: warm under Shah (fellow CENTO member); cooled sharply after Iranian Revolution (1979) — Shia-Sunni sectarian tension spilled over.
  • Left SEATO in 1972 after it failed to support Pakistan in 1971 war. Left CENTO in 1979.
Nuclear Programme
  • Bhutto launched programme seriously after 1971 defeat ("they have the bomb, we will eat grass"). Dr A.Q. Khan returned from Netherlands with centrifuge technology.
  • May 1998 — Pakistan tested 5 nuclear devices at Chagai (Balochistan) in response to India's tests. Massive international sanctions followed.
  • Difficulties as a nuclear state: US sanctions cut off military and economic aid. Financial crisis deepened. International isolation. Pressure to sign NPT (Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty) and CTBT. A.Q. Khan network controversy exposed (2004).

🔥 Very Hot Topography — Landforms of Pakistan +
The main regions
  • Northern Mountains: Karakoram, Himalayas, Hindu Kush. K2 (8,611m) is the world's second highest peak. Glaciers feed the major rivers. Sparse population due to difficult terrain.
  • Western Mountains & Plateaus: Sulaiman, Kirthar, Makran ranges. Balochistan Plateau — high elevation, arid, sparsely populated. Problems: poor soil, lack of water, difficult communications.
  • Upper Indus Plain: Punjab heartland — fertile, well-irrigated, densely populated. Major agricultural zone. Flat land ideal for canal irrigation.
  • Lower Indus Plain: Sindh — also fertile but hotter and drier. Relies heavily on Indus irrigation. Prone to flooding.
  • Flood Plains: Active flood plain (flooded annually), old flood plain (terraced land above flood level). Features: meanders, levees, ox-bow lakes.
  • Balochistan Plateau farming problems: Thin, rocky soil; very low rainfall; no perennial rivers; only Karez systems provide irrigation; steep slopes cause erosion; poor road connectivity.

🔥 Very Hot Climate — Monsoon & Western Depressions +
The Monsoon
  • Causes: In summer, the Asian landmass heats up faster than the Indian Ocean. Low pressure develops over the land. Moist wind is sucked northward from the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal.
  • Route in Pakistan: Enters from the Bay of Bengal into India first (June), reaches Pakistan by late June/July. The Arabian Sea branch enters through Sindh. Total duration: July–September.
  • Rainfall distribution: Heaviest in the north-east (Murree, Kashmir — over 1500mm). Decreasing westward and southward. Karachi gets very little monsoon rain. Balochistan is almost entirely missed.
  • Why NW Pakistan gets less rainfall: Mountains block moist air; by the time the monsoon reaches this far, most moisture has already fallen; distance from moisture source; rain-shadow effect of mountain ranges.
Western Depressions
  • Low-pressure systems that move east from the Mediterranean and Caspian Sea in winter (December–March).
  • Bring winter rainfall and snowfall to northern Pakistan and the western highlands.
  • Vitally important for wheat growing (winter crop needs this moisture) and for recharging groundwater and glaciers.
  • Karachi occasionally receives rain from these systems when they dip south.

🔥 Very Hot Agriculture — Major Crops & Irrigation +
The four major crops
  • Wheat: Rabi (winter) crop. Sown October–November, harvested March–April. Grown across Punjab and Sindh. Needs cool moist conditions for germination, warm dry conditions at harvest. Pakistan's staple food crop.
  • Rice: Kharif (summer) crop. Sown May–June, harvested October–November. Requires standing water — grown in canal-irrigated areas of Punjab and Sindh. Basmati rice from Punjab is a major export earner.
  • Cotton: Kharif crop. Grown mainly in Punjab and Sindh. Needs long, hot summers and little rain at boll-opening. Pakistan's most important cash crop — raw material for textile industry (Pakistan's biggest industry). Over 60% of export earnings from cotton and textiles.
  • Sugarcane: Kharif crop. Needs heavy irrigation, high temperatures, fertile soil. Grown mainly in Punjab. Raw material for Pakistan's sugar industry.
Waterlogging & Salinity
  • Waterlogging causes: Over-irrigation raises the water table. Seepage from unlined canals. Poor drainage in flat areas. Water table rises above root zone — crops drown.
  • Salinity causes: When waterlogged land dries, water evaporates leaving dissolved salts behind. Saline water used for irrigation. Natural salts in soil accumulate over time.
  • Effects: Reduced crop yields, land abandonment, soil structure destroyed.
  • Solutions: SCARP (Salinity Control and Reclamation Project) — tube wells to pump out water. Lining of canals. Better drainage networks. Crop rotation. Use of gypsum to treat saline soils.
Irrigation systems
  • Canals: World's largest irrigation network. Headworks and barrages divert river water into main canals → branch canals → distributaries → watercourses → fields.
  • Tube wells: Pump groundwater. Important in areas between canals. Can cause waterlogging if overused.
  • Karez (qanat): Traditional underground channels used in Balochistan. Gravity-fed — no energy needed. Tap water table at higher elevation, channel it underground to lower fields. Sustainable but limited in capacity.
  • Major dams: Tarbela (Indus), Mangla (Jhelum) — store water for dry season irrigation and hydroelectric power.

🔥 Very Hot Power Resources & Load Shedding +
Types of power
  • Hydroelectric Power (HEP): Tarbela and Mangla dams provide ~30% of electricity. Advantages: renewable, no fuel cost, clean. Disadvantages: siltation of reservoirs, seasonal variation (low in dry winters), high construction cost, displacement of communities.
  • Thermal (oil/gas): Largest share of electricity. Burns natural gas, oil, or coal. Advantages: reliable, can increase output quickly. Disadvantages: expensive fuel, air pollution, gas reserves depleting.
  • Nuclear: KANUPP (Karachi), CHASNUPP (Chashma). Small contribution currently but government expanding. Advantages: large output, no carbon emissions. Disadvantages: high cost, radioactive waste, safety concerns, international pressure.
  • Solar & Wind: Rapidly growing. Sindh and Balochistan have excellent solar potential. Jhimpir wind corridor in Sindh. Advantages: free fuel, renewable, no pollution. Disadvantages: intermittent, storage problems, high initial cost.
Load shedding — causes & effects
  • Causes: Demand grows faster than supply. Transmission losses (old, inefficient grid). Electricity theft widespread. Circular debt — power companies can't pay fuel suppliers. Seasonal variation in HEP. Lack of investment in new generation capacity.
  • Effects: Industry loses production; businesses use expensive generators. Agricultural tube wells can't operate — crop yields fall. Schools and hospitals affected. Daily life disrupted — up to 12–18 hours of load shedding at peak.
  • Difficulties in providing power to all areas: Remote mountain communities too far from grid; difficult terrain makes power line construction expensive; low population density makes the investment uneconomical; communities rely on kerosene and firewood instead.

🔥 Very Hot Population — Birth Rate, Death Rate, DTM & Migration +
Key concepts
  • Birth rate reasons (high): Cultural preference for large families; sons provide security in old age; lack of family planning access/awareness; early marriage; low female education and employment; agricultural families need labour; religious attitudes.
  • Birth rate solutions: Female education; improving women's status; family planning services; incentives for smaller families; economic development.
  • Death rate reasons (declining): Improved healthcare, vaccination programmes, cleaner water, better nutrition, improved sanitation, better medical training.
  • Effects of high birth rate: Strain on schools, hospitals, housing, jobs, food supply. Large dependent youth population. Urbanisation pressure. Government spending stretched.
Demographic Transition Model (DTM)
  • Stage 1: High birth rate, high death rate — small population. Pre-industrial societies.
  • Stage 2: High birth rate, falling death rate — rapid population growth. Pakistan is in this stage transitioning to Stage 3.
  • Stage 3: Falling birth rate, low death rate — growth slows.
  • Stage 4: Low birth rate, low death rate — stable population. Developed countries.
  • Pakistan sits between Stage 2 and Stage 3: death rates have fallen significantly; birth rates are falling but still high.
Population pyramids
  • Wide base = high birth rate. Pakistan's pyramid has a very wide base — large young population.
  • Rapid tapering = high death rate in older age groups. Small elderly population.
  • How to describe: comment on the shape (wide/narrow base), the gender balance, which age groups are largest, and what this tells you about birth and death rates.
Migration
  • Rural to urban (internal): Push factors — poverty, unemployment, lack of services, floods, drought. Pull factors — jobs, hospitals, schools, entertainment, higher wages.
  • Overseas migration: To Middle East (construction, labour), UK (established Pakistani diaspora), USA. Remittances are a major source of income for Pakistan — billions of dollars annually. Negative: brain drain, family separation, social disruption in origin villages.
  • Katchi Abadis: Informal settlements/slums in Pakistan's cities created by rapid rural-urban migration. Problems: no clean water, no sewage, overcrowding, lack of schools, insecure land tenure. Self-help schemes aim to improve conditions.

🔥 Very Hot Industry, Trade & Transport +
Major industries
  • Cotton textiles: Pakistan's largest industry. Concentrated in Karachi, Faisalabad, Lahore. Uses domestically grown cotton. Produces yarn, cloth, garments. Over 60% of export earnings. Problems: old machinery, energy costs, water use, competition from Bangladesh and China.
  • Sugar: Large industry in Punjab and Sindh near sugarcane growing areas (to minimise transport of bulky raw material). Seasonal — operates during harvest period.
  • Iron & Steel: Pakistan Steel Mills in Karachi (near port for imported iron ore and coal). Supplies construction and engineering industries.
  • Factors affecting industrial location: Raw materials nearby; water supply; labour availability; transport links; market access; energy; government policy (EPZs, tax incentives).
Trade & Balance of Payments
  • Main exports: Cotton textiles and garments, rice, leather goods, sports goods (Sialkot), surgical instruments (Sialkot).
  • Main imports: Petroleum, machinery, chemicals, edible oil, fertiliser, iron/steel.
  • Trade deficit: Imports > Exports. Pakistan consistently runs a trade deficit — imports cost more than exports earn. Causes: low value of raw material exports, expensive oil imports, limited industrial exports.
  • Balance of Payments: Overall picture — trade deficit partially offset by remittances from overseas Pakistanis. But still often negative.
Transport
  • Railways: Colonial-era network; connects major cities. Problems: old stock, slow, under-investment, accidents. Important for freight and long-distance passengers.
  • Motorways: M1–M9 motorway network, modern and well-maintained. Connects Peshawar to Karachi. Boosted trade and logistics significantly.
  • Problems in mountainous areas: Landslides block roads; high construction cost; steep gradients; thin population makes investment hard to justify; snow closure in winter; earthquake damage. Many communities accessible only by jeep tracks or on foot.
